How Race Timing Chips Work: RFID, Antenna Mats, and Why Accuracy Matters

~2,000 words · Target: "how race timing chips work RFID" · Publish: Day 150

Every time you cross a timing mat at a race, an invisible conversation happens between your timing chip and the reader below your feet. Here's the full technical picture — and why it matters for your bib clip choice.

RFID Basics

RFID stands for Radio Frequency Identification. Your timing chip contains a tiny antenna and a microchip — no battery needed. When you step on a timing mat, the mat broadcasts a radio signal that powers your chip's antenna. The chip then transmits your unique ID back to the reader. This all happens in milliseconds.

Common Timing Systems

Champion Chip (ankle/shoe): One of the oldest systems. Uses low-frequency 125kHz RFID. Attached to your shoe or ankle with a Velcro strap. Very reliable. No bib interaction.

MyLaps BibTag: Modern bib-mounted system using UHF (860-960MHz). Thin tag stuck directly to the back of your race bib. Higher read rate than shoe-mounted systems. Used by many major US races.

Chronotrack D-Tag: Disposable bib-mounted UHF tag. Similar to MyLaps but typically lower cost for race directors. Single-use — you don't return it.

What Can Go Wrong

Timing misreads are rare but they happen: damaged chip, covered antenna, runner cutting a corner and missing the mat, or — theoretically — electromagnetic interference near the chip.

This last point is where bib clip choice becomes relevant. Bib-mounted timing chips (BibTag, D-Tag) sit on the race bib — exactly where bib clips also attach. If a clip generates a magnetic field, it's millimeters from the RFID antenna.

The Magnetic Question

Neodymium magnets in magnetic bib clips generate 50-200 gauss at the clip face. While RFID uses radio waves (not static magnetic fields), strong magnets near an antenna can potentially detune it — reducing read range or reliability.

Twist-lock clips eliminate this variable entirely. POM polymer is electromagnetically inert. Zero magnetic field. Zero possible interaction with any RFID frequency.
