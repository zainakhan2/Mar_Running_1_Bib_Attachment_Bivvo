Do Magnetic Bib Clips Interfere With Timing Chips? Here's What We Know

~2,000 words · 7 min read · Target: "magnetic bib clips timing chip" · Publish: W-5

The Question That Won't Go Away

If you've ever searched for bib clips on Amazon, you've seen this debate: "Will these magnets mess up my timing chip?" It's the single most common concern about magnetic race bib clips. And it's legitimate — your timing chip is the only thing between you and an official finish time.

How Race Timing Chips Work

Modern race timing uses RFID technology. A small transponder contains an antenna and microchip. When you cross a timing mat, the mat emits a radio signal that powers the transponder, which transmits your unique ID back to the reader.

Common systems: Champion Chip (ankle, 125kHz), MyLaps BibTag (bib-mounted, 860-960MHz UHF), Chronotrack D-Tag (bib-mounted, UHF), IPICO (shoe, 125kHz).

The Science of Magnetic Interference

Neodymium magnets in bib clips generate 50–200 gauss at the clip face. This drops to ~5 gauss at 1 inch. RFID uses radio frequency electromagnetic waves, not static magnetic fields — in theory, different physics principles.

However: strong magnets can potentially affect antenna tuning of an RFID tag in very close proximity. Bib-mounted timing tags sit directly where magnetic clips also sit — millimeters from the RFID antenna.

What the Data Says

No published peer-reviewed study definitively proves or disproves interference. Thousands of runners use magnetic clips without reported issues. No timing company has published controlled testing proving magnets are safe near RFID tags.

Bottom line: Magnetic clips are probably fine for most runners. But "probably fine" ≠ "guaranteed safe." For Boston qualifiers, age group awards, and PR attempts — that uncertainty matters.

The Zero-Risk Alternative

Twist-lock clips use a bayonet-mount mechanism with zero magnets, zero magnetic field, and zero possible interaction with RFID frequencies. POM (Delrin®) polymer is non-ferromagnetic, non-conductive, and electromagnetically inert. It's not "low risk" — it's physically impossible to interfere.
